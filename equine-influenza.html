Equine Influenza (EI)

Equine Influenza is a highly contagious viral respiratory disease affecting horses, donkeys, mules, and other equids. Characterized by rapid transmission, fever, and coughing, it causes significant economic impact due to disruption of training and competition schedules. While rarely fatal, it predisposes horses to secondary bacterial infections.

Key Characteristics

Incubation

1-3 days

Distribution

Worldwide (except Australia, NZ, Iceland)

Recovery

Minimum 3 weeks rest required

Mortality

Rarely fatal

Etiology

Causative Agents

  • Two subtypes of influenza A viruses:
    • Influenza A/equine-1 (H7N7)
    • Influenza A/equine-2 (H3N8)
  • Family: Orthomyxoviridae
  • Related to but distinct from human and avian influenza viruses
  • Constantly evolving through antigenic drift
  • H3N8 now dominant worldwide (H7N7 not detected since 1980)
Epidemiology
  • Occurs worldwide except Australia, New Zealand and Iceland
  • Transmission:
    • Direct contact with infected animals
    • Inhalation of respiratory aerosols
    • Fomites (brushes, tack, clothing, equipment)
  • Very short incubation period (1-3 days)
  • Short duration of infectivity (7-10 days)
  • All age groups susceptible, especially 2-6 months
  • Outbreaks can occur year-round
  • Transport and crowding increase transmission risk
  • Immunity after infection lasts 6-12 months
Pathogenesis
  • Viral inhalation and attachment to respiratory epithelial cells via hemagglutinin spikes
  • Fusion with epithelial cells and release into cytoplasm
  • Initial replication in nasopharyngeal mucosa
  • Within 3-7 days, virus spreads throughout respiratory tract
  • Infection causes:
    • Death of ciliated epithelial cells
    • Edema and inflammation
    • Loss of mucociliary clearance
  • Bronchopneumonia may develop from secondary bacterial infections
Clinical Signs
  • Sudden onset and rapid spread in outbreaks
  • High fever (up to 41°C)
  • Dry, hacking cough progressing to moist cough
  • Serous nasal discharge → mucopurulent with secondary infection
  • Depression and anorexia
  • Muscle pain and reluctance to move
  • Abnormal lung sounds (crackles, wheezes)
  • Edema of distal limbs and trunk
  • Enlarged, painful submandibular lymph nodes
  • Pneumonia in severe cases
  • Signs more severe in unvaccinated horses
Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods

  • Presumptive diagnosis:
    • Rapid spread of respiratory infection
    • High fever, depression, cough
  • Definitive diagnosis:
    • Viral isolation (nasal swabs)
    • RT-PCR (most sensitive method)
    • Hemagglutination inhibition (HI) test
    • Influenza A antigen detection (lateral flow tests)
    • Serology (paired samples showing rising antibody titers)
  • Nasopharyngeal swabs should be taken early in disease
Differential Diagnosis
  • Equine Herpesvirus (EHV-1 and EHV-4): Similar respiratory signs, neurological or abortion signs
  • Equine Viral Arteritis (EVA): Respiratory signs, edema, abortion
  • Strangles (Streptococcus equi): Purulent nasal discharge, abscessed lymph nodes
  • Equine Rhinitis Virus: Mild respiratory signs
  • Inflammatory Airway Disease (IAD): Chronic cough, poor performance
  • Recurrent Airway Obstruction (RAO): Chronic respiratory condition

Disease Progression

Virus Inhalation
Respiratory Tract Infection
Epithelial Damage
Secondary Bacterial Invasion

The virus enters through inhalation and infects respiratory epithelial cells, causing damage that impairs the mucociliary clearance mechanism. This damage creates opportunities for secondary bacterial infections, potentially leading to bronchitis or pneumonia if not properly managed.

Treatment

Rest

Minimum 3 weeks complete rest with gradual return to work

Anti-inflammatories

NSAIDs for fever reduction and pain relief

Antibiotics

For secondary bacterial infections or pneumonia

Hydration

Ensure adequate water intake and electrolyte balance

Prevention and Control

Vaccination

Regular vaccination with updated strains

Quarantine

Isolate new horses for 2+ weeks

Hygiene

Thorough cleaning and disinfection of stables and equipment

Biosecurity

Limit horse movement during outbreaks

Surveillance

Monitor for early signs of disease

Stress Reduction

Minimize transportation and overcrowding

Vaccination Protocol
  • Vaccine types:
    • Inactivated vaccines
    • Canarypox vectored vaccines
    • Immune stimulating complex (ISCOM) vaccines
  • Schedule:
    • Primary course: 2 doses 4-6 weeks apart
    • First booster: 6 months after second dose
    • Subsequent boosters: Every 6-12 months depending on risk
  • Competition requirements:
    • FEI requires vaccination within 6 months of competition
    • Many racing authorities have specific requirements
  • Vaccine effectiveness reduced due to antigenic drift - regular updates needed
Complications
  • Secondary bacterial pneumonia
  • Myositis (muscle inflammation)
  • Myocarditis (heart muscle inflammation)
  • Chronic bronchitis
  • Poor performance syndrome
  • Immunosuppression leading to other infections
  • Economic losses from cancelled events and training disruption

Important Note

Inadequate rest after infection is a major risk factor for development of chronic respiratory problems. Horses should have a minimum of 3 weeks rest after clinical recovery, with a gradual return to work over several weeks.

Economic Impact

Equine influenza outbreaks can cause significant economic losses. The 2007 Australian outbreak resulted in cancellation of events, disrupted breeding seasons, and cost an estimated $1 billion in direct and indirect costs.

Risk Factors

Young horses (2-3 years), horses in frequent transport, racehorses, and show horses are at highest risk. Vaccination history, immune status, and management practices significantly affect susceptibility and disease severity.
