Peste des Petits Ruminants (PPR)

Also known as: Goat plague, Pseudo-rinderpest, Kata (Nigeria), Pneumoenteritis complex

PPR is an acute or subacute contagious viral disease of sheep and goats characterized by fever, conjunctivitis, erosive stomatitis, gastroenteritis, and pneumonia. Goats are more susceptible and more severely affected than sheep.

Key Characteristics

Mortality Rate

50-90% in acute cases

Distribution

West & Central Africa, Middle East, Asia

Most Affected

Young animals (4-8 months)

Zoonotic

No reported human cases

Etiology
  • PPR virus (Morbillivirus)
  • Family: Paramyxoviridae
  • Single-stranded RNA virus
  • Affinity for lymphoid and epithelial tissues
  • Closely related to rinderpest virus
Epidemiology
  • Common in West and Central Africa and the Middle East
  • Hosts: Sheep and goats (goats more susceptible)
  • Transmission:
    • Direct close contact
    • Ocular discharge
    • Nasal discharge
    • Oral secretions
    • Feces
    • Inhalation of aerosolized droplets
  • No carrier stage
Pathogenesis
  • Virus penetrates retropharyngeal mucosa
  • Viral aggregation leads to viremia (fever)
  • Viremia causes damage to lymphoid and respiratory systems
  • Infected cells undergo necrosis
  • Death results from severe diarrhea and dehydration
Clinical Signs - Acute Form

Mainly in goats, incubation period: 3–6 days

  • High fever (>40°C)
  • Restlessness, dull hair coat, dry muzzle
  • Congested mucous membranes
  • Coughing
  • Oral mucosa coated with whitish exudate
  • Fetid mouth odor
  • Vesicles and necrotic lesions in oral mucosa
  • Serous to mucopurulent nasal discharge
  • Necrotic areas on nasal mucosa
  • Conjunctivitis with crusty lesions
  • Severe non-hemorrhagic diarrhea → dehydration
  • Progression to bloody diarrhea
  • Death due to hypothermia within 10 days
Clinical Signs - Subacute Form

More common in sheep

  • Slight fever
  • Nasal discharge
  • Mild oral erosions
  • Intermittent diarrhea
  • Recovery is common
Predisposing Factors
  • Poor nutritional status
  • Stress of movement/transport
  • Concurrent bacterial or parasitic infections
  • Overcrowding
  • Poor sanitation
Postmortem Lesions
  • Necrotic and inflammatory lesions in mouth and GIT
  • Sunken eyeballs
  • Dirty-white false membrane in mouth
  • Erosions on gums, palate, and cheeks
  • Erosions on esophageal lining
  • Abomasum: regulatory outline erosions oozing blood
  • Small intestine: blood streaks, duodenum/ileum erosions
  • Peyer's patches: necrosis and severe ulceration
  • Large intestine: congestion around valves
  • Posterior colon and rectum show "zebra/tiger stripes"
  • Lungs: bronchointerstitial pneumonia
  • Generalized lymphadenopathy
Differential Diagnosis
  • Orf - causes mouth lesions similar to PPR
  • CCPP (Contagious Caprine Pleuropneumonia) - respiratory distress with pneumonia
  • Gastrointestinal helminth infection - diarrhea and weight loss
  • Bluetongue - similar signs but different transmission
  • Foot and Mouth Disease - vesicular lesions
Diagnosis
  • History (contact with PPR animals) and clinical signs
  • Confirmatory laboratory tests:
    • Virus isolation
    • Paired sera test
    • Monoclonal antibody test
    • Competitive ELISA
    • AGID (Agar Gel Immunodiffusion) test
    • PCR
    • Serum neutralization test
Treatment

Supportive treatment only:

  • Antibiotics: oxytetracycline, penicillin-streptomycin (prevent secondary infection)
  • Anti-diarrheals: kaolin-pectin mixture
  • Vitamin supplements: Vitamin B complex, A, C
  • Fluid therapy: oral rehydration salts or IV fluids
  • Blood transfusion: if severe anemia present
  • Local oral care: remove exudate, apply mild antiseptic

Prevention and Control

Vaccination

Vaccinate susceptible animals with live attenuated vaccine

Quarantine

Isolate new animals for at least 21 days

Grazing Management

Avoid communal grazing and borrowing of breeding males

Movement Planning

Vaccinate pastoral herds at least one month before expected movement

Biosecurity

Implement strict farm biosecurity measures

Surveillance

Establish disease monitoring and reporting systems

Global Eradication Program

PPR is targeted for global eradication by 2030 through a joint program by FAO and OIE. The strategy focuses on vaccination campaigns, surveillance, and control measures.

Economic Impact

PPR causes annual losses of $1.5-2 billion globally, affecting over 300 million small farming families who depend on small ruminants for their livelihood.
