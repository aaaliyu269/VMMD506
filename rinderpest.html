Rinderpest (Cattle Plague)

Officially eradicated in 2011 - First animal disease eradicated globally

Rinderpest was a highly contagious viral disease affecting cloven-hoofed animals, especially cattle. Characterized by fever, necrotic stomatitis, gastroenteritis, and high mortality. It was one of the most devastating animal diseases in history before its global eradication.

Key Characteristics

Mortality Rate

Up to 100% in susceptible herds

Status

Eradicated globally in 2011

Most Affected

Cattle (most severely affected)

Zoonotic

No record of human infection

Etiology
  • Rinderpest virus (RPV)
  • Family: Paramyxoviridae
  • Genus: Morbillivirus
  • Single-stranded RNA virus
  • Very fragile, rapidly inactivated by heat and light
  • Remains viable longer in cold weather and shade
  • Strains: Asia 1 and lineages 1, 2, and 3
Transmission
  • Close contact between infected and non-infected animals
  • Contaminated feed and water
  • Inhalation of infectious aerosols
  • Secretions and excretions:
    • Nasal discharge
    • Ocular discharge
    • Feces
  • No carrier state
  • No vector involved
  • Indirect transmission via fomites possible
Incubation Period
  • 4-5 days
  • Can range from 3-15 days depending on strain virulence
Morbidity and Mortality
  • Morbidity and mortality can reach 100% in susceptible herds
  • Young animals more susceptible than adults
  • Certain breeds more susceptible than others
Clinical Signs

Prodromal Phase

  • Fever up to 41Â°C
  • Restlessness
  • Erect hair coat
  • Loss of appetite
  • Lacrimal and ocular discharges

Mucosal Phase

  • Pinpoint necrotic foci on mucous membranes
  • Sloughing of necrotic tissues exposing hemorrhagic basal layers
  • Persistent mucosal, lacrimal, and ocular discharges
  • Increased thirst
  • Soft feces progressing to watery consistency

Diarrheic Phase

  • Feces becomes dark, fluid, and foul-smelling
  • Contains mucus, necrotic debris, and streaks of blood
  • Profuse or projectile diarrhea
  • Progressive weight loss
  • Ocular/nasal discharges change to purulent
  • Stringy salivation
  • Flies attracted to mouth and anus
  • Eyelashes matted together
  • Conjunctivitis, uveitis, and keratitis
  • Cracking of muzzle skin
  • Abdominal pain
  • Secondary bacterial infections
  • Enlarged lymph nodes
  • Abortion in pregnant animals
Subclinical Form
  • Seen in other ruminants and pigs
  • Anorexia
  • Inflammation of buccal mucosa
  • Small pustules around neck, flanks, and inside thighs
Postmortem Lesions
  • Most remarkable lesions in gastrointestinal tract
  • Mouth: necrotic epithelium sloughed off, red ulcerations
  • Small intestine: erosion of Peyer's patches in jejunum and ileum
  • Mesenteric lymph nodes swollen and edematous
  • Hemorrhagic gastroenteritis
  • Zebra-striping in colon and rectum
  • Gallbladder distension
  • Dehydration and emaciation
Diagnosis
  • History: highly necrotizing stomatitis and pneumoenteritis
  • Clinical signs: acute mucosal and diarrheic phases
  • Laboratory tests:
    • IgA gel test
    • Immunodiffusion test
    • Passive hemagglutination test
    • Immunofluorescence test
    • Monoclonal antibody test
    • PCR
    • Virus isolation
Treatment
  • No effective treatment available
  • No effort should be made to treat infected animals
  • Euthanasia recommended for infected animals
  • Strict quarantine measures essential
Prevention and Control
  • Vaccination: Tissue culture rinderpest vaccine (TCRV)
  • Adequate quarantine in outbreak areas
  • Slaughter and safe disposal (burying/burning) of infected animals
  • Movement restrictions
  • Disinfection of premises and equipment
  • Surveillance and monitoring
  • Public awareness campaigns
Differential Diagnosis
  • Bovine malignant catarrhal fever
  • Foot and mouth disease
  • CBPP (Contagious bovine pleuropneumonia)
  • Bovine viral diarrhea
  • Infectious bovine rhinotracheitis
  • ECF (East Coast fever)
  • Salmonellosis

Global Eradication

  • Rinderpest is the first animal disease to be eradicated globally
  • Last case reported in Kenya in 2001
  • Officially declared eradicated by OIE in 2011
  • Success due to:
    • Effective vaccine (TCRV)
    • Strong international cooperation
    • Surveillance and monitoring programs
    • Strict quarantine measures
    • Public awareness campaigns
  • Lessons learned from rinderpest eradication are being applied to other diseases like PPR

Historical Significance

Economic Impact

Rinderpest caused famines and economic devastation throughout history, including the African rinderpest pandemic of 1887-1897 that killed 90% of cattle in sub-Saharan Africa

Veterinary Science

Led to the establishment of the first veterinary school in Lyon, France in 1762 to combat rinderpest

International Cooperation

Rinderpest control led to the establishment of OIE (World Organisation for Animal Health) in 1924

Vaccine Development

The development of TCRV by Walter Plowright earned him the World Food Prize in 1999

Important Note

Although eradicated, rinderpest virus samples are still maintained in high-security laboratories. Strict biosecurity protocols are essential to prevent accidental release.

Legacy

The successful eradication of rinderpest demonstrates that global eradication of animal diseases is possible with international cooperation, effective vaccines, and sustained efforts.
