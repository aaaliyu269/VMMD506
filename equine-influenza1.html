Equine Influenza (EI)

Equine Influenza is a highly contagious respiratory disease of horses, donkeys, mules and other equidae. Although rarely fatal, it causes significant economic losses due to disruption of training and competition schedules.

Key Characteristics

Incubation Period

1-3 days

Distribution

Worldwide (except Australia, NZ, Iceland)

Recovery Time

Minimum 3 weeks rest required

Mortality

Rarely fatal

Etiology
  • Two subtypes of influenza A viruses:
    • Influenza A/equine-1 (H7N7)
    • Influenza A/equine-2 (H3N8)
  • Orthomyxoviruses
  • Related to but distinct from human and avian influenza viruses
  • Constantly evolving through antigenic drift
Epidemiology
  • Occurs worldwide except in Australia, New Zealand and Iceland
  • Transmission:
    • Direct contact with infected animals
    • Inhalation of aerosols
    • Fomites (brushes, clothing, equipment)
  • Very short incubation period (1-3 days)
  • Short duration of infectivity (7-10 days)
  • All age groups susceptible, especially 2-6 months
  • Outbreaks can occur year-round
  • Transport and crowding favor spread
Pathogenesis
  • Viral inhalation and attachment to respiratory epithelial cells via hemagglutinin spikes
  • Fusion with epithelial cells and release into cytoplasm
  • Initial replication in nasopharyngeal mucosa
  • Within 3-7 days, virus spreads throughout respiratory tract
  • Infection causes death of epithelial cells, edema, and loss of mucociliary clearance
  • Bronchopneumonia may develop from secondary bacterial infections
Clinical Signs
  • Sudden onset and rapid spread in outbreaks
  • Fever (up to 41°C)
  • Dry, hacking cough progressing to moist cough
  • Serous nasal discharge initially, becoming mucoid with secondary infection
  • Depression and anorexia
  • Occasionally muscle pain and reluctance to move
  • Abnormal lung sounds
  • Edema of distal limbs and trunk
  • Submaxillary lymph nodes may be enlarged and painful
  • Pneumonia may develop in severe cases
Diagnosis
  • Presumptive diagnosis based on:
    • Rapid spread of respiratory infection
    • High fever
    • Depression
    • Cough
  • Definitive diagnosis:
    • Viral isolation (nasal swabs)
    • Hemagglutination inhibition (HI) test
    • Influenza A antigen detection (patient-side kit)
    • RT-PCR
    • Serology (paired samples showing rising antibody titers)
Differential Diagnosis
  • Equine Herpesvirus (EHV-1 and EHV-4)
  • Equine Viral Arteritis (EVA)
  • Strangles (Streptococcus equi)
  • Equine Rhinitis Virus
  • Inflammatory Airway Disease (IAD)
  • Recurrent Airway Obstruction (RAO)

Treatment

  • Supportive care and rest (minimum 3 weeks)
  • NSAIDs for high fever (>40°C) and pain relief
  • Antibiotics when fever exceeds 3-4 days or when pneumonia develops
  • Bronchodilators in severe cases
  • Good nursing care and nutrition
  • Isolation of affected horses
  • Adequate hydration

Prevention and Control

Vaccination

Regular vaccination with updated strains

Biosecurity

Hygienic management practices

Quarantine

Isolate new horses for 2+ weeks

Movement Control

Restrict movement during outbreaks

Surveillance

Monitor for early signs of disease

Disinfection

Thorough cleaning of stables and equipment

Vaccination Against Equine Influenza

  • Vaccines available:
    • Inactivated vaccines
    • Canarypox vectored vaccines
    • Immune stimulating complex (ISCOM) vaccines
  • Vaccination schedule:
    • Primary course: 2 doses 4-6 weeks apart
    • First booster: 6 months after second dose
    • Subsequent boosters: Every 6-12 months depending on risk
  • Competition requirements:
    • Many equestrian organizations require up-to-date vaccination
    • FEI requires vaccination within 6 months of competition
  • Vaccine effectiveness reduced due to antigenic drift - regular updates needed

Economic Impact

Equine influenza outbreaks can cause significant economic losses due to cancellation of events, disruption of training schedules, and veterinary costs. The 2007 Australian outbreak cost an estimated $1 billion.

Important Note

Inadequate rest after infection is a major risk factor for development of chronic respiratory problems. Horses should have a minimum of 3 weeks rest after clinical recovery.
