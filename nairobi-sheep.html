Nairobi Sheep Disease (NSD)

Nairobi Sheep Disease is a highly infectious acute and fatal viral disease affecting sheep and goats. Characterized by high fever, hemorrhagic gastroenteritis, and mortality rates exceeding 90%, it's primarily transmitted by ticks.

NSD is endemic in parts of Africa and poses a significant threat to small ruminant populations. The virus is closely related to the Crimean-Congo Hemorrhagic Fever virus, which affects humans.

Key Characteristics

Mortality

Equal to or greater than 90%

Distribution

Endemic in Africa

Vector

Rhipicephalus appendiculatus ticks

Vaccine

No vaccine currently available

Etiology

Causative Agent

  • Nairobi Sheep Disease Virus (NSDV)
  • Closely related to Crimean-Congo Hemorrhagic Fever Virus
  • Belongs to the genus Nairovirus, family Bunyaviridae
  • Enveloped, single-stranded RNA virus
Epidemiology
  • Geographic Distribution: Primarily found in East Africa
  • Hosts: Sheep and goats (primary hosts)
  • Vector: Rhipicephalus appendiculatus (brown ear tick)
  • Reservoir: Sheep and possibly wildlife
  • Transmission: Tick bites (no animal-to-animal transmission)
  • Maintenance: Tick-vertebrate-tick cycle in nature
  • Seasonal occurrence correlates with tick activity
Pathogenesis
  • Tick bite deposits virus into host
  • Virus replicates in local lymph nodes
  • Enters bloodstream (viremia)
  • Attaches to gastrointestinal epithelial cells
  • Causes hemorrhagic damage to GI tract
  • Leads to hemorrhages and necrosis
  • Massive endothelial damage causes systemic effects
Clinical Signs
  • High fever (41-42Â°C)
  • Anorexia (loss of appetite)
  • Severe depression
  • Bloody diarrhea (hemorrhagic gastroenteritis)
  • Vomiting
  • Nasal discharge
  • Abdominal pain
  • Mortality: 90-100% in susceptible flocks
  • Death typically occurs 2-7 days after symptom onset
Postmortem Lesions
  • Hemorrhagic intestine
  • Enlarged mesenteric lymph nodes
  • Abomasal hemorrhages
  • Gastrointestinal mucosa ulceration
  • Splenomegaly
  • Petechiae on heart and other organs
  • Lungs may show congestion and edema
Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods

  • History and clinical signs
  • Viral isolation (from blood or tissues)
  • ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)
  • Histopathology (tissue examination)
  • Gross lesions examination
  • RT-PCR (Reverse Transcription Polymerase Chain Reaction)
  • Serological tests (detection of antibodies)

Transmission Cycle

Infected Tick Vector
Sheep/Goat Infection
Virus Replication
Uninfected Ticks Feed

The virus is maintained in nature through a tick-vertebrate-tick cycle. Infected ticks transmit the virus to sheep and goats during feeding. After replication in the host, uninfected ticks become infected when feeding on viremic animals, completing the transmission cycle.

Differential Diagnosis
  • PPR (Peste des Petits Ruminants): Causes fever, diarrhea, and high mortality
  • Coccidiosis: Diarrhea (sometimes bloody) with weakness
  • Blue Tongue: Hemorrhagic lesions, transmitted by biting midges
  • Salmonellosis: Septicemia, diarrhea, possible high mortality
  • Enterotoxemia: Sudden death with hemorrhagic enteritis
  • Rift Valley Fever: Also causes hemorrhagic symptoms and high mortality
Treatment

Management Approach

  • No specific antiviral treatment
  • Supportive therapy is essential:
    • Fluid therapy (e.g., Darrow's solution)
    • Electrolyte balance maintenance
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent secondary infections
  • Anti-inflammatory drugs
  • Isolation of infected animals
  • Nursing care and comfortable environment
  • Euthanasia may be necessary in severe cases

Prevention and Control

Tick Control

Regular acaricide application, pasture management, and tick habitat reduction

Quarantine

Isolate newly purchased animals for at least 30 days before introducing to the flock

Hygiene

Maintain clean housing, proper waste disposal, and disinfection protocols

Movement Control

Restrict animal movement from endemic to free areas, especially during outbreaks

Important Note

Nairobi Sheep Disease is a notifiable disease in many countries. Any suspected cases must be reported to veterinary authorities immediately. Early detection and implementation of control measures are crucial for outbreak management.

Zoonotic Potential

While primarily affecting ruminants, the closely related Crimean-Congo Hemorrhagic Fever virus causes severe disease in humans. Although NSDV itself isn't considered a significant human pathogen, precautions should be taken when handling infected animals or tissues.
