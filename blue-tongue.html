Blue Tongue Disease (BTV)

Blue Tongue is an infectious, non-contagious viral disease affecting domestic and wild ruminants, primarily transmitted by Culicoides midges. The disease is characterized by fever, inflammation of mucous membranes, and in severe cases, cyanosis of the tongue giving the disease its name.

While sheep are most severely affected, cattle serve as important reservoir hosts. The disease has significant economic impacts due to trade restrictions and production losses.

Key Characteristics

Mortality

2% to 90% (varies by strain)

Distribution

Africa, Middle East, Southern Europe, Americas

Vector

Culicoides species (biting midges)

Vaccine

Inactivated vaccine available

Etiology

Causative Agent

  • Blue Tongue Virus (BTV)
  • Member of genus Orbivirus, family Reoviridae
  • Double-stranded RNA virus with 10 segments
  • At least 27 distinct serotypes exist
  • Serotypes vary in virulence and geographic distribution
  • Virus is resistant to lipid solvents and stable at pH 6-8
Epidemiology
  • Geographic Distribution: Determined by presence of Culicoides vectors
  • Primary Regions: East Africa, Middle East, Southern Europe, and parts of the Americas
  • Transmission: Biting midges (Culicoides species)
  • Seasonality: Highest incidence in late summer and autumn
  • Hosts: Sheep (most susceptible), cattle, goats, deer, and wild ruminants
  • Reservoir: Cattle (develop high viremia with minimal signs)
  • Virus does not spread directly between animals
Pathogenesis
  • Virus inoculation by infected midge
  • Replication in regional lymph nodes
  • Primary viremia (virus in bloodstream)
  • Infection of endothelial cells and vascular damage
  • Secondary viremia (high viral load)
  • Widespread vascular injury leading to:
    • Edema
    • Hemorrhage
    • Ischemia
    • Necrosis
  • Virus crosses placenta in pregnant animals
Postmortem Lesions
  • Petechial hemorrhages in various organs
  • Ecchymoses at base of pulmonary artery
  • Subcutaneous and intramuscular edema
  • Hydrothorax and hydropericardium
  • Pneumonia and pericarditis
  • Ulceration of oral mucosa and tongue
  • Congestion and hemorrhage in GI tract
  • Muscle degeneration (skeletal and cardiac)

These lesions are considered pathognomonic for Blue Tongue Disease

Clinical Signs in Sheep

Acute Form

  • High fever (40.5°C to 41°C)
  • Reluctance to move
  • Edema of lips, nose, face, eyelids, and ears
  • Congestion of oral and nasal mucosa
  • Serous nasal discharge becoming mucopurulent
  • Frothy oral discharge
  • Ulceration of tongue
  • Swollen cyanotic protruding tongue
  • Excessive salivation
  • Torticollis (head turning)
  • Lameness and stiffness
  • Death in 6-8 days after onset

Peracute Cases

  • Sudden death without prior signs
  • Severe pulmonary edema
  • Frothy exudate from nostrils
  • Death by aspiration
Clinical Signs in Cattle
  • Usually subclinical (reservoir hosts)
  • Fever in some cases
  • Increased respiratory rate
  • Lacrimation and salivation
  • Oral vesicles and ulcers
  • Hyperesthesia (increased sensitivity)
  • Vesicular and ulcerative dermatitis
  • Coronitis (inflammation of coronary band)
  • Nasal discharge
  • Reduced milk production
  • Abortion or malformed offspring

Fetal Effects (Both Species)

  • Abortion
  • Mummified fetus
  • Congenital malformations:
    • Hydranencephaly
    • Porencephaly
    • Cerebellar hypoplasia
    • Arthrogryposis
  • Live-born animals with neurological defects

Transmission Cycle

Infected Midge Vector
Infection of Ruminant
Viremia Development
Uninfected Midges Feed

The virus circulates between ruminant hosts and Culicoides midges. Infected midges transmit the virus during blood meals. After replication in the host, uninfected midges become infected when feeding on viremic animals. The cycle is dependent on environmental factors that affect midge populations.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Methods

  • History and clinical signs (especially in sheep)
  • Virus isolation (blood, spleen, or lymph nodes)
  • ELISA (detection of antibodies or antigens)
  • Immunofluorescent test (tissue sections)
  • Viral neutralization test (serotype identification)
  • RT-PCR (detection of viral RNA)
  • Immunoperoxidase test (tissue detection)
  • Postmortem lesions examination
Differential Diagnosis
  • Foot and Mouth Disease: Vesicular lesions in mouth and feet
  • Malignant Catarrhal Fever: Ocular and nasal discharge, corneal opacity
  • Contagious Ecthyma (Orf): Pustular lesions around mouth
  • Photosensitization: Similar facial edema but seasonal
  • Peste des Petits Ruminants: Severe diarrhea and pneumonia
  • Rift Valley Fever: Abortions and hepatic necrosis
  • Epizootic Hemorrhagic Disease: Similar transmission and signs
Treatment

Management Approach

  • No specific antiviral treatment
  • Supportive care is essential:
    • Provide soft, palatable feed
    • Shelter from sun and wind
    • Reduce stress and movement
  • Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent secondary infections
  • Fluid therapy for dehydrated animals
  • Topical treatments for oral lesions
  • Euthanasia for severely affected animals
Prevention & Control
  • Vector Control:
    • Insecticide treatment of animals
    • Environmental management to reduce breeding sites
    • Stabling animals during peak midge activity
  • Vaccination:
    • Inactivated vaccines (safer but shorter immunity)
    • Live attenuated vaccines (longer immunity but risk to pregnant animals)
    • Vaccines are serotype-specific
  • Surveillance: Monitoring and reporting systems
  • Movement Restrictions: During outbreaks
  • Biosecurity: Quarantine of new animals

Prevention Strategies

Vector Management

Use insecticides, reduce breeding sites, and stable animals during peak midge activity

Vaccination

Implement vaccination programs with appropriate serotype vaccines

Movement Control

Restrict movement of animals from endemic to free areas

Surveillance

Establish monitoring programs for early detection of outbreaks

Important Note

Blue Tongue is a notifiable disease in most countries. Any suspected cases must be reported to veterinary authorities immediately. The disease has significant trade implications, and outbreaks can lead to international trade restrictions.

Zoonotic Potential

Blue Tongue virus does not infect humans and is not considered a zoonotic disease. However, similar viruses in the same family (Orbiviruses) can occasionally cause human illness, so standard precautions should be taken when handling infected animals or samples.

Climate Change Impact

Climate change is expanding the geographic range of Culicoides vectors, leading to the spread of Blue Tongue to previously unaffected regions. Surveillance and control programs need to adapt to these changing patterns.
