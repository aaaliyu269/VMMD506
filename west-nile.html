West Nile Virus Encephalitis

Mosquito-borne viral disease affecting horses, birds, and humans

West Nile Virus (WNV) is a mosquito-borne flavivirus that primarily affects birds but can cause encephalitis in horses and humans. Since its introduction to North America in 1999, it has become endemic across the continent, posing a significant risk to equine health.

Key Characteristics

Primary Hosts

Birds (reservoir hosts)

Transmission

Mosquito-borne (Culex species)

Equine Mortality

30-40% in clinical cases

Zoonotic

Yes (affects humans)

Etiology
  • Virus: West Nile Virus (WNV)
  • Family: Flaviviridae
  • Genus: Flavivirus
  • Belongs to Japanese Encephalitis virus antigenic complex
  • Single-stranded RNA virus
  • Enveloped virus
Epidemiology
  • Distribution: Endemic in United States, Europe, West and Central Asia, Africa, Oceania, and Middle East
  • Primary Reservoirs: Birds (especially corvids - crows, jays)
  • Dead-end Hosts: Horses, humans, and other mammals
  • Transmission:
    • Primarily by Culex species mosquitoes
    • Bird-mosquito-bird cycle maintains the virus
    • No direct horse-to-horse or horse-to-human transmission
  • Seasonal occurrence (late summer to early fall)
Pathogenesis
  • Virus inoculation through mosquito bite
  • Replication at site of infection
  • Spread to regional lymph nodes
  • Primary viremia
  • Infection of endothelial cells and replication in organs
  • Secondary viremia
  • Crossing of blood-brain barrier
  • Infection of central nervous system
  • Inflammation and neuronal damage
Clinical Signs in Horses
  • Ataxia (most common sign)
  • Rear limbs more often involved
  • Muscular fasciculations (especially around nose and lips)
  • Transient hypersensitivity to touch and sound
  • Fever (may or may not be present)
  • Weakness or paralysis of hindlimbs
  • Recumbency (inability to stand)
  • Seizures or coma (less common)
  • Behavioral changes (depression, aggression)
  • Twitching of muzzle and face muscles
  • Improvement may be noticed within days in some cases
Diagnosis
  • History and clinical signs (especially neurological signs)
  • Serology:
    • IgM capture ELISA (most common test)
    • Plaque reduction neutralization test (PRNT)
  • Virus isolation (difficult, low sensitivity)
  • RT-PCR (cerebrospinal fluid or brain tissue)
  • Postmortem examination:
    • Non-suppurative encephalomyelitis
    • Perivascular cuffing
    • Neuronal necrosis
    • Gliosis
Differential Diagnosis
  • Eastern Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEE)
  • Western Equine Encephalomyelitis (WEE)
  • Venezuelan Equine Encephalomyelitis (VEE)
  • Rabies
  • Equine Herpes Myeloencephalopathy (EHM)
  • Botulism
  • Tetanus
  • Equine Protozoal Myeloencephalitis (EPM)
  • Trauma or spinal cord injury

Transmission Cycle

1. Bird-Mosquito Cycle

Mosquitoes acquire the virus by feeding on infected birds (reservoir hosts). The virus replicates in the mosquito.

2. Transmission to Horses/Humans

Infected mosquitoes transmit the virus to horses, humans, and other mammals during blood feeding.

3. Dead-end Hosts

Horses and humans develop low-level viremia insufficient to infect mosquitoes, making them dead-end hosts.

Treatment

  • No specific antiviral treatment
  • Supportive care:
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs, corticosteroids)
    • Fluid therapy
    • Nutritional support
    • Good nursing care
  • Management of recumbent horses:
    • Soft bedding
    • Frequent turning
    • Bladder management
  • Anticonvulsants if seizures occur
  • Antibiotics to prevent secondary infections
  • Physical therapy for recovery

Prevention and Control

Vaccination

Core vaccine for horses in endemic areas

Mosquito Control

Reduce breeding sites and use insecticides

Stable Management

Keep horses indoors during peak mosquito activity

Repellents

Use approved equine mosquito repellents

Surveillance

Monitor dead birds and mosquito populations

Public Health

Community-wide mosquito control programs

Vaccination Information

  • Vaccines available:
    • Inactivated whole virus vaccine
    • Recombinant canarypox vectored vaccine
    • DNA vaccine
  • Vaccination schedule:
    • Primary series: 2 doses, 3-6 weeks apart
    • First booster: 6 months after second dose
    • Annual revaccination
    • In high-risk areas, semi-annual vaccination may be recommended
  • Vaccine efficacy: Approximately 95% in preventing disease

Zoonotic Potential

  • WNV is a significant human health concern
  • Transmission:
    • Primarily through mosquito bites
    • Rare cases through blood transfusion, organ transplantation, and maternal-fetal transmission
  • Human disease spectrum:
    • Asymptomatic (80% of cases)
    • West Nile Fever (mild flu-like illness)
    • Neuroinvasive disease (encephalitis, meningitis)
  • Prevention in humans:
    • Mosquito repellents
    • Protective clothing
    • Avoid outdoor activities during peak mosquito times
    • Window screens
    • Community mosquito control

Important Note

Vaccination is the most effective way to protect horses from West Nile Virus. Horses in endemic areas should be vaccinated annually before mosquito season begins.

Recovery and Prognosis

Horses that survive the acute phase of WNV infection often recover completely, though some may have residual neurological deficits. Recovery may take weeks to months.
