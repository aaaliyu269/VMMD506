Equine Encephalitides

Arthropod-borne viral diseases affecting the central nervous system of horses

Equine encephalitides are serious viral diseases transmitted by mosquitoes that cause inflammation of the brain (encephalitis) in horses. The three main types in the Western Hemisphere are Eastern Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEE), Western Equine Encephalomyelitis (WEE), and Venezuelan Equine Encephalomyelitis (VEE).

Key Characteristics

Most Severe

Eastern Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEE)

Transmission

Mosquito-borne

Mortality

EEE: 75-90%, WEE: 20-30%, VEE: 40-80%

Zoonotic

All types can infect humans

Etiology
  • Family: Togaviridae
  • Genus: Alphavirus
  • Types:
    • Eastern Equine Encephalomyelitis (EEEV)
    • Western Equine Encephalomyelitis (WEEV)
    • Venezuelan Equine Encephalomyelitis (VEEV)
  • Single-stranded RNA viruses
  • Enveloped viruses
  • Formerly classified as Arboviruses group A
Epidemiology
  • Geographic Distribution:
    • EEE: Eastern North America, Caribbean
    • WEE: Western North America
    • VEE: Central and South America
  • Transmission: Mosquito vectors
  • Reservoirs:
    • EEE: Birds (primary reservoir)
    • WEE: Birds and small mammals
    • VEE: Rodents (sylvatic cycle)
  • Horses and humans are dead-end hosts
  • Seasonal occurrence (summer and fall)
Viral Strains

EEEV

  • One virus strain with two variants:
    • North American variant (more pathogenic)
    • South American variant

WEEV

  • Least virulent in horses and humans
  • WEEV complex contains six viruses

VEEV

  • Six antigenic subtypes (I-VI)
  • Subtypes I A/B and C pathogenic to horses and humans
Transmission
  • EEEV:
    • Primary vector: Culiseta melanura
    • Bridge vectors: Aedes, Culex, Ochlerotatus species
    • Transmission cycle: Bird-mosquito-bird
  • WEEV:
    • Primary vector: Culex tarsalis
    • Transmission cycle: Bird-mosquito-bird
    • Can overwinter in reptiles
  • VEEV:
    • Vectors: Aedes, Anopheles, Culex, Mansonia species
    • Sylvatic cycle: Rodent-mosquito-rodent
    • Epizootic cycle: Horse-mosquito-horse
Clinical Signs
  • Diseases clinically indistinguishable
  • More severe in unvaccinated horses
  • Initial signs: Fever, anorexia, depression
  • Neurological signs appear after 5 days:
    • Encephalitis
    • Hypersensitivity to stimuli
    • Involuntary muscle movements
    • Impaired vision
    • Aimless wandering
    • Head pressing
    • Circling
  • Advanced signs:
    • Inability to swallow
    • Ataxia and paresis
    • Paralysis
    • Excitement
    • Intense pruritus
    • Convulsions
    • Paddling when recumbent
  • Death in few days (especially with EEE)
Diagnosis
  • Presumptive diagnosis based on:
    • Clinical presentation
    • Associated epidemiologic features
  • Definitive diagnosis:
    • Viral isolation (brain tissue)
    • RT-PCR detection of viral RNA
    • Serology (four-fold rise in antibody titer)
  • Common serological tests:
    • Hemagglutination Inhibition (HI)
    • Complement Fixation (CF)
    • Serum Neutralization (SN)
    • Antibody-capture ELISA
  • CSF analysis: Increased cellularity and protein
  • Necropsy: Histopathological lesions in gray matter

Comparison of Equine Encephalitides

Eastern (EEE)

  • Mortality: 75-90%
  • Course: Short and severe
  • Vector: Culiseta melanura
  • Reservoir: Birds
  • Distribution: Eastern North America

Western (WEE)

  • Mortality: 20-30%
  • Course: Longer than EEE
  • Vector: Culex tarsalis
  • Reservoir: Birds and mammals
  • Distribution: Western North America

Venezuelan (VEE)

  • Mortality: 40-80%
  • Course: Rapid progression
  • Vector: Multiple mosquito species
  • Reservoir: Rodents (sylvatic)
  • Distribution: Central and South America

Treatment

  • No specific antiviral treatment
  • Supportive care:
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
    • Fluid therapy
    • Nutritional support
    • Good bedding and nursing care
  • Symptomatic treatment:
    • Muscle relaxants (e.g., diazepam)
    • Anticonvulsants (e.g., pentobarbital)
    • Protection from self-trauma
  • Isolation of affected horses
  • Euthanasia may be necessary in severe cases

Prevention and Control

Vaccination

Core vaccine for horses in endemic areas

Mosquito Control

Reduce breeding sites and use insecticides

Stabling

Keep horses indoors during peak mosquito activity

Screens and Fans

Use physical barriers in stables

Surveillance

Monitor mosquito populations and disease activity

Public Awareness

Educate about personal protection measures

Zoonotic Potential

  • All three viruses can infect humans
  • Transmission through mosquito bites
  • EEE causes most severe human disease with high mortality
  • Human cases often preceded by equine outbreaks
  • Prevention:
    • Mosquito repellents
    • Protective clothing
    • Avoid outdoor activities during peak mosquito times
    • Community mosquito control programs

Important Note

Vaccination is the most effective prevention method. Horses in endemic areas should be vaccinated annually, preferably before mosquito season begins.

Climate Change Impact

Changing climate patterns are expanding the geographic range of mosquito vectors, potentially increasing the risk of encephalitis outbreaks in new areas.
