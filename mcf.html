Malignant Catarrhal Fever (MCF)

Also known as Malignant Head Catarrh

Malignant Catarrhal Fever is an acute, sporadic, infectious disease of cattle and other bovids characterized by low morbidity but very high mortality. It is caused by gammaherpesviruses and has a complex epidemiology involving reservoir hosts.

Key Characteristics

Mortality Rate

Approaches 100% in affected cattle

Distribution

Worldwide

Reservoir Hosts

Wildebeest, sheep

Vaccine

No effective vaccine available

Etiology
  • Wildebeest-associated form: Alcelaphine Herpesvirus-1 (AHV-1)
  • Sheep-associated form: Ovine Herpesvirus-2 (OvHV-2)
  • Gammaherpesviruses in the family Herpesviridae
  • Double-stranded DNA viruses
Transmission
  • Nasal and ocular secretions
  • Feces of infected animals
  • Close contact with reservoir hosts
  • Sheep-associated form: transmitted from sheep to cattle (no cattle-to-cattle transmission)
  • Wildebeest-associated form: transmitted from wildebeest to cattle, especially during calving season
  • No evidence of insect vector involvement
Forms & Clinical Signs

Per-acute form

  • Common in deer
  • Sudden death without prior signs

Acute form

  • Fever (40-42°C)
  • Depression
  • Enlarged superficial lymph nodes
  • Serous nasal and ocular discharges → mucopurulent
  • Erosion of buccal papillae
  • Ophthalmia; corneal opacity; hypopyon
  • Inflammation/erosive lesions in URT mucosa
  • Nasal mucosa ulceration, crusting of muzzle
  • Salivation
  • Conjunctivitis
  • Matting of hair around eyes/nose
  • Ulceration of perineum, vulva, coronary band, interdigital skin, teats
  • CNS signs: excitability, hyperesthesia, muscle tremors, convulsions
  • Severe weight loss
  • Incubation period: ~2 weeks

Sub-acute/Chronic form

  • Less severe signs
  • Longer course, progressive wasting
Gross Lesions
  • Generalized congestion of almost all organs
  • Erosive lesions throughout gastrointestinal tract
  • Lymph node enlargement
  • Necrotizing vasculitis in multiple organs
  • Corneal opacity and ocular inflammation
  • Hemorrhagic cystitis
  • Meningoencephalitis in cases with CNS signs
Clinical Pathology
  • Lymphocytosis followed by lymphopenia
  • Increased liver enzymes
  • Proteinuria
  • Anemia in chronic cases
Diagnosis
  • History: outbreaks in nearby herds, wildlife contact
  • Clinical signs: characteristic multisystemic disease
  • Serological tests:
    • Viral neutralization test
    • Competitive inhibition ELISA
    • Conventional ELISA
    • Immunofluorescent test
  • Virus isolation (difficult)
  • Immunoperoxidase test (tissue samples)
  • PCR (most sensitive method)

Differential Diagnosis

  • Bovine Viral Diarrhea (BVD): Mucosal disease form with erosions
  • Infectious Bovine Rhinotracheitis (IBR): Respiratory signs, conjunctivitis
  • Foot and Mouth Disease: Vesicular lesions, fever
  • Rinderpest: Similar gastrointestinal signs (now eradicated)
  • Bluetongue: Similar in sheep, but different transmission
  • Mucosal diseases: Similar erosive lesions

Treatment

  • No specific antiviral treatment
  • Supportive care:
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
    • Fluid therapy
    • Antibiotics to prevent secondary infections
    • Ocular treatments for eye lesions
  • Isolation of affected animals
  • Euthanasia is often necessary due to poor prognosis

Prevention and Control

Separation

Separate cattle from reservoir hosts (sheep, wildebeest)

Biosecurity

Implement strict biosecurity measures on farms

Wildlife Management

Prevent contact with wildebeest during calving season

Herd Management

Avoid mixing sheep and cattle on same premises

Quarantine

Quarantine new animals before introduction

Surveillance

Monitor herds for early signs of disease

Important Note

MCF is often a fatal disease with no effective treatment. Prevention through management practices that limit contact between cattle and reservoir hosts is the most effective control strategy.

Economic Impact

MCF causes significant economic losses in affected herds due to high mortality rates, especially in areas where cattle and reservoir hosts coexist. Outbreaks can be devastating to individual producers.
